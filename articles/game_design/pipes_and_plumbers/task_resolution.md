## Task Resolution

The most basic element in any game system is the task resolution heuristic. Simply put, when a player character attempts to do something that may or may not work, how do you determine whether the action succeeded for failed? Most tabletop games use dice to add randomness to the process, and a set of numerical character properties to shape or interpret the results.

### The Check

In Pipes and Plumbers, task resolution is handled using the Check mechanic. To perform a check, roll two six-sided dice and add their results together. Then, find any attributes (see below a future post) or other bonuses or penalties that affect the success or failure of the action, and add or subtract those to or from the dice result. Finally, compare the final result to a Difficulty rating that quantifies how difficult the action is to successfully perform.

A few notes on the selection of two six-sided dice. First, six-siders are common, so people who aren't long-time gamers won't need to go out and track down a game store to start playing. As for the numeric consequences, that deserves a section to itself.

#### By The Numbers

Rolling two six-sided dice (heretofore abbreviated as "2d6" for brevity) has certain numerical properties, which we will use going forward to shape the rest of our system. The minimum result is 2, from rolling two ones, and the maximum result is 12, from rolling two sixes. So the range is 2-12, a total of eleven different possible results.

Since we are adding dice together, though, those possibilities are not all equally likely! In fact, rolling a 2 will only happen one time in 36, on average; rolling a 12 is equally uncommon. On the other hand, rolling the average result of 7 will happen six times in 36, or one time in six. So the probability of getting a particular result starts out very low at the bottom of the range, gets much higher near the middle, and ends up low again at the top.

What this means is that dice rolls (and check results) will be much more reliable than if we were just rolling one larger die. There is still a reasonable chance to roll really well (or, of course, really poorly!) but if a character can complete an action on an average roll, then most of the time they're going to successfully complete the action on a check, with a small but reasonable chance of failure (and a similar chance of doing exceptionally well).

#### Bonuses and Penalties

There is one other effect worth mentioning from choosing 2d6 as the basis for our check. Remember how results around the average are more common than near the minimum or maximum? That also means that bonuses and penalties have a greater effect when the success and failure are closely balanced. If you need to roll an 8 or better to succeed, then getting a +1 bonus is going to increase your odds of success much more than if you needed a roll of 11.

The other result of this is that, for very difficult actions, getting a bonus has a much larger effect than getting a penalty, in terms of your probability of success. Likewise, for very easy actions, a bonus doesn't help much, but a penalty can add a significant chance of failure, so beware.
