## Hit Points and Flower Points

Last time, we covered the basic flow of combat in the Pipes and Plumbers mini-system. Today we'll go into a little more detail on keeping score - victory, defeat, and the resources that can make the difference.

Recall that each creature has a set of attribute values, which are a quantitative measure of that creature's general strengths and weaknesses. Two of those were Hit Points and Flower Points. Strictly speaking, however, those attributes actually represent a creature's maximum Hit Points and maximum Flower Points.

For our purposes, keep in mind that a creature has a maximum Hit Points value, which is an attribute, ranges from 1 to 6 (more or less), and doesn't change often. Each creature also has a current hit points value, which is their health at that moment, ranges from 0 to their maximum hp, and changes quite often. Likewise, a creature has both a maximum Flower points value and a current value.

### Hit Points and Damage

Hit points are a common feature in both digital and tabletop games, but we're not trying to re-invent the wheel here. In Pipes and Plumbers, hit points represent the creature's ability to take and endure damage.

When a creature is hit with an successful attack or special ability, it loses hit points as noted by the ability. When the creature's current hit points have reached zero (or below!), the creature is Knocked Out and cannot take any actions until revived.

### Flower Points and Damage

Flower points are the yin to hit points's yang. Rather than representing the endurance or defensive capabilities of a creature, they represent its ability to dish out hurt. In Pipes and Plumbers, a creature (usually) must spend flower points to use special abilities, such as powerful jump attacks or spells.

When a creature uses an ability, it must spend a number of current flower points as noted by the ability. If it does not have enough points, it cannot use the ability.

### Rest And Recovery

Since we want the game to be fast-paced, we're going to use more liberal rules about recovery than in a more traditional game system. First, at the end of a battle (assuming the player characters won!) they can automatically revive any fallen party members, although the revived are only restored to 1 hit point. Second, they can choose to take rests to restore lost hit points and spent flower points.

#### Ten Minute Rest

If the characters take a ten minute rest, they each recover one spent flower point (of course, up to their maximum fp value). Ten-minute rests cannot be taken consecutively - if the players want more rest before the next scene, they can take an hour rest instead.

#### Hour Rest

After resting for an hour, each character recovers all of their spent flower points.

#### Overnight Rest

After resting overnight, each character recovers all of their lost hit points and spent flower points.

### Using Recovery Items

Some powerups allow a character to quickly recover lost hit points or spent flower points. Of course, players should be more careful to manage this resource, since used powerups are not recovered after a rest.

### Reviving Fallen Allies

Finally, a few words on reviving allies that have been knocked out. First, allies are automatically revived at the end of battle (assuming their friends win, of course!), so as long as the entire group is not knocked out, there is no need to worry about permanent death or injury.

If players wish to revive a character in the middle of a battle, there are two ways to do so. First, some powerups allow a character to immediately revive a fallen ally. If a powerup is not available, then any adjacent creature may attempt to revive a fallen ally as an action during their turn. To do so, they must succeed at a check (Difficulty 8). After two unsuccessful attempts, the ally can be revived as an action without requiring a check. The attempts need not be made consecutively or by the same ally.
